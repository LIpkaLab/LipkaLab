Current Projects

  • ComplexTraits: There are emerging theory in genetic architecture that combine quantitative genetics (in the Lipka Lab’s wheelhouse) with a functional understanding of molecular mechanisms (not in the Lipka Lab’s wheelhouse). We argue that current publicly available statistical and simulation pipelines are too simple to test for the quantitative evidence of these emerging theories. Therefore, we are currently developing such pipelines.
  • Perennial sorghum: Perennialized variants of sorghum has a potential to offer greater food security while providing less damage to soil than annual crops. We are developing genomic prediction pipelines to help expedite genetic gains in perennial experimental crosses of S.bicolor x S.halepense. We will soon evaluate how virtual plants can be used to obtain better training populations for genomic prediction.
  • Applying AI/ML to quantitative genetics: We hypothesize that AI can complement traditional quantitative genetics approaches. Our first dedicated project towards evaluating is to the task of GWAS: while existing mixed models approaches have provided substantial insight into identifying genomic regions with large-effect loci, we are investigating how well a random forest approach we developed can identify such genomic regions that are in linkage disequilibrium with rare variants.
  • Using -omic data to estimate GxE in maize hybrids: It has clearly been shown that gene expression differs under environmental stresses. Therefore, we are determining ways that this differential expression can be incorporated into multi-kernel genomic selection models for genomic prediction of maize hybrids grown across many environments.
